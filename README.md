# Artificial hybridization among Vinca accessions provides high segregation in important ornamental traits
## Manuscript CBAB-2026-5590 submitted to Crop Breeding and Applied Biotechnology
### Abstract: Vinca is a widely cultivated ornamental plant valued for its broad adaptation and continuous flowering. This study aimed to develop assess the segregation of ornamental traits in F₁ and F₂ populations of Catharanthus roseus obtained through controlled hybridization and evaluate the segregation of ornamental traits, including flower color, petal arrangement, receptacle color, plant growth habit, and leaf density. Five crosses involving contrasting parental genotypes were performed under protected condition, and the resulting populations were characterized using UPOV descriptors and RHS flower color standards. Controlled hybridization effectively produced 25, 18, 20, 40, and 36 F₁ hybrids in Crosses 1–5, respectively. Considerable phenotypic variation was perceived, remarkably for flower color and petal arrangement. Crosses involving distinct floral pigmentation engendered diverse phenotypes in the F₁ generation, including rare orange shades, while selfing of F₁ plants amplified segregation in the F₂ generation, follow-on in sophisticated frequencies of red and magenta flowers. Chi-square investigates assisted Mendelian inheritance for plant growth habit, leaf density, receptacle color, and petal arrangement, with segregation ratios matching expected 1:1 and 3:1 proportion. A dominance hierarchy was identified for petal arrangement and plant growth habit. On the other hand, flower color revealed more complex segregation arrangements, revealing the involvement of multiple genetic factors and gene interactions. The populations yielded comprise precious breeding resources and provide new insights into the inheritance of ornamental behavior in C. roseus.
